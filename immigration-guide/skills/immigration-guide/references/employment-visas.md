## 1. H-1B Specialty Occupation

### What it is
Temporary work visa for specialty occupations requiring at least a bachelor's degree.
Employer must sponsor. 6-year maximum (with extensions possible if green card process started).

### Key facts for self-navigating applicants
- **You cannot file H-1B yourself** — your employer files Form I-129 on your behalf.
- Annual cap: 65,000 + 20,000 for U.S. master's degree holders.
- Cap-exempt employers: universities, nonprofits affiliated with universities, government research orgs.
- Electronic registration (lottery) opens in March each year for October 1 start.
- If selected in lottery, employer files the full petition.

### What YOU should know and prepare
- Your degree must relate to the specialty occupation. USCIS often challenges the
  connection — especially for general business degrees in tech roles.
- Get your foreign credentials evaluated (if applicable) by a NACES-member service
  like WES, ECE, or SpanTran BEFORE your employer files.
- Keep copies of all documents your employer submits — you're entitled to them.
- **Changing employers:** You can transfer H-1B to a new employer. The new employer
  files a new I-129. You can start working for the new employer as soon as the
  petition is received by USCIS (portability under AC21).
- **If you lose your job:** You have a 60-day grace period (or until your I-94 expires,
  whichever is shorter) to find a new sponsor, change status, or leave.

### Common pitfalls
- Employer doesn't file the Labor Condition Application (LCA) correctly — delays everything.
- Degree-occupation mismatch triggers RFEs — especially in IT consulting.
- Working at third-party worksites adds complexity (Simeio Solutions scrutiny).
- Dependents (H-4): spouse can get H-4 status. H-4 EAD is available if H-1B holder
  has an approved I-140 or is in 7th+ year extension.

### When to get an attorney
- Employer won't share petition documents with you.
- You have a criminal record.
- You're in removal proceedings.
- Your case involves a third-party worksite arrangement.

## 2. L-1 Intracompany Transfer

### What it is
For employees transferring from a foreign office to a U.S. office of the same company.
- L-1A: managers/executives (up to 7 years)
- L-1B: specialized knowledge workers (up to 5 years)

### Key facts
- Employer files Form I-129 with L supplement.
- No annual cap, no lottery.
- Must have worked for the foreign entity for 1 continuous year in the past 3 years.
- Blanket L-1 petitions available for large companies with multiple transferees.

### What YOU should prepare
- Evidence of the qualifying relationship between foreign and U.S. entities
  (same employer, parent, subsidiary, affiliate).
- Detailed description of your role abroad and your proposed U.S. role.
- For L-1A: evidence of managerial/executive duties (not just a title).
- For L-1B: evidence of specialized knowledge (proprietary systems, processes).

### Common pitfalls
- L-1B "specialized knowledge" is vaguely defined — high denial rate.
- Small companies face extra scrutiny about whether the U.S. office is real.
- New office L-1 petitions (for companies without existing U.S. operations) get
  only 1-year approval and heavy documentation requirements.

## 3. O-1 Extraordinary Ability

### What it is
For individuals with extraordinary ability in sciences, arts, education, business,
or athletics. No annual cap.

### Self-petition note
O-1 is employer-sponsored (Form I-129), but the "employer" can be an agent
or even your own company. This makes it quasi-self-sponsorable.

### Eligibility (meet 3 of 8 criteria OR a major one-time achievement)
1. Awards or prizes for excellence
2. Membership in associations requiring outstanding achievement
3. Published material about you in professional/major media
4. Judging the work of others in your field
5. Original contributions of major significance
6. Authorship of scholarly articles
7. Employment in a critical/essential capacity for distinguished organizations
8. High salary or remuneration relative to others in the field

### What to prepare
- A detailed advisory opinion from a peer group or labor organization.
- Evidence packets organized by criterion — 3+ exhibits per criterion claimed.
- A detailed itinerary or description of the work to be performed.
- Letters from experts in your field attesting to your extraordinary ability.

### Common pitfalls
- Claiming too many criteria weakly instead of fewer criteria strongly.
- Advisory letter that's generic rather than specific to your achievements.
- Confusing O-1A (sciences/business/education/athletics) with O-1B (arts) —
  different standards.

## 4. TN (USMCA/NAFTA)

### What it is
Work authorization for Canadian and Mexican citizens in specific professions listed
in the USMCA treaty. Renewable in 3-year increments, no cap on renewals.

### Key facts
- **Canadians:** Apply directly at a U.S. port of entry — no prior USCIS petition needed.
- **Mexicans:** Must obtain a TN visa at a U.S. consulate first, OR apply at a port of entry
  (same as Canadians since 2024 USMCA update — verify current policy).
- Limited to professions on the USMCA list (engineers, accountants, scientists,
  management consultants, etc.).
- Must have the degree or credentials specified for your profession.
- No dual intent — TN is officially a nonimmigrant visa, which complicates
  green card applications.

### Common pitfalls
- Job title doesn't match a listed USMCA profession — reclassify the role.
- Management consultant is heavily scrutinized — must show actual consulting
  (not just a management role).
- Dual intent issues: filing I-140 while on TN can complicate TN renewals,
  though many practitioners manage this successfully.

## 5. E-2 Treaty Investor

### What it is
For nationals of treaty countries who invest a substantial amount in a U.S. business.

### Key facts
- No minimum investment amount specified by law, but USCIS expects a "substantial"
  investment — typically $100K+ for a small business, depending on the business type.
- Must be a national of a treaty country (check State Department list).
- Applied for at a U.S. consulate (not USCIS in most cases).
- Renewable indefinitely in 2-year increments.
- Does NOT lead directly to a green card.

### What to prepare
- Business plan showing the investment is real and the business is viable.
- Evidence of funds and their lawful source.
- Evidence the investment is "at risk" (committed to the business, not sitting in escrow).
- Proof you'll develop and direct the enterprise (not a passive investor for small investments).

## 6. PERM Labor Certification

### What it is
The first step in most employer-sponsored green card processes (EB-2 and EB-3).
The employer proves to the Department of Labor that no qualified U.S. workers
are available for the position.

### Key facts for applicants
- Your employer drives this process. You don't file anything yourself.
- Involves recruitment steps: job posting, ads in newspapers/journals, job fairs, etc.
- Takes 6-18+ months depending on backlogs and audit risk.
- If the DOL audits the application, add 6-12+ months.
- After PERM approval, employer files I-140, then you file I-485 (or consular process).

### What YOU should know
- Your resume and qualifications must match the PERM job requirements exactly.
  Don't embellish — inconsistencies trigger audits.
- You can't have been involved in the recruitment process.
- If you change employers before I-140 is approved, you generally must restart PERM.
- After I-140 approval + 180 days on pending I-485, you can change employers
  (AC21 portability) as long as the new job is in a "same or similar" occupation.

## 7. Employment-Based Green Cards

### Categories
| Category | Who qualifies | Self-petition? |
|---|---|---|
| EB-1A | Extraordinary ability | Yes |
| EB-1B | Outstanding professor/researcher | No (employer sponsors) |
| EB-1C | Multinational manager/executive | No (employer sponsors) |
| EB-2 | Advanced degree or exceptional ability | Only with NIW |
| EB-2 NIW | National Interest Waiver | Yes (self-petition) |
| EB-3 | Skilled workers, professionals, other | No (employer + PERM) |

### Priority dates and visa bulletin
- After I-140 approval, your "priority date" is established.
- Check the monthly Visa Bulletin (State Department) to see if your priority date is current.
- If current: file I-485 (adjust status in U.S.) or do consular processing.
- If not current: wait. For India and China EB-2/EB-3, waits can be years to decades.

### Important protections
- **I-140 portability:** Once I-140 is approved for 180+ days, it remains valid even
  if the employer withdraws it (unless fraud or material misrepresentation).
- **Priority date retention:** You can keep your priority date and apply it to a
  new I-140 from a different employer.
- **H-1B extensions beyond 6 years:** If I-140 is approved or PERM has been pending
  for 365+ days, you can extend H-1B in 1-year or 3-year increments.
