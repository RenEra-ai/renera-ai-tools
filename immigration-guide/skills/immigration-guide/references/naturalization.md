## 1. Eligibility Requirements

### Standard path (INA §316)
You must meet ALL of the following:
- **Age:** At least 18 years old at time of filing
- **LPR status:** Have been a lawful permanent resident (green card holder) for at least 5 years
  - Reduced to 3 years if married to and living with a U.S. citizen spouse
- **Continuous residence:** Lived in the U.S. for at least 5 years (or 3 years) as an LPR
  - Trips outside the U.S. of 6+ months but less than 1 year create a rebuttable
    presumption of broken continuity
  - Trips of 1+ year break continuous residence (unless you filed N-470 before departing)
- **Physical presence:** Physically present in the U.S. for at least 30 months
  out of the 5-year period (or 18 months out of 3 years)
- **State residence:** Lived in the state where you're filing for at least 3 months
- **Good moral character:** During the statutory period (5 or 3 years before filing
  through the oath ceremony)
- **English language:** Ability to read, write, and speak basic English
  - Exempt if 50+ years old and 20+ years as LPR ("50/20 rule")
  - Exempt if 55+ years old and 15+ years as LPR ("55/15 rule")
  - Medical exemption available with Form N-648
- **Civics knowledge:** Pass a test on U.S. history and government (see section 3)
  - 65+ years old with 20+ years as LPR get a simplified test
- **Attachment to the Constitution:** Willing to support the U.S. Constitution

### Early filing
You can file N-400 up to 90 days before meeting the continuous residence requirement
(i.e., 4 years and 9 months as LPR, or 2 years and 9 months for 3-year rule).

## 2. The N-400 Application Process

### Step-by-step

1. **Confirm eligibility** using the checklist above.

2. **Gather documents:**
   - Green card (both sides)
   - Passport and travel records for all trips outside the U.S. during the statutory period
   - Tax returns for the past 5 years (or 3 years)
   - If applicable: marriage certificate, divorce decrees, court records for any
     criminal history, Selective Service registration
   - Two passport-style photos

3. **Complete Form N-400** — available at uscis.gov. Can file online or by mail.
   - Answer every question honestly and completely
   - Disclose ALL arrests, citations, and charges — even if dismissed, expunged, or sealed
   - Disclose ALL trips outside the U.S.

4. **Pay filing fee** — check uscis.gov for current fee (subject to change).
   Fee waiver available (Form I-912) based on income/benefits.

5. **Biometrics appointment** — fingerprints and photos at a USCIS Application Support Center.

6. **Interview** at your local USCIS field office (see section 3).

7. **Decision:** approved, denied, or continued (if USCIS needs more information).

8. **Oath ceremony** — take the Oath of Allegiance. You receive your Certificate
   of Naturalization. You are now a U.S. citizen.

### Timeline
- Processing times vary widely by field office — 6 to 18+ months from filing to oath.
- Check current processing times at egov.uscis.gov/processing-times for your field office.

## 3. The Naturalization Interview and Tests

### What to expect
- Conducted by a USCIS officer at a field office
- The officer reviews your N-400 answers under oath
- You take the English and civics tests during the interview
- Bring: interview notice, green card, passport, state-issued ID, any documents
  listed in the notice

### English test
Three components:
1. **Reading:** Read 1 of 3 sentences correctly out loud
2. **Writing:** Write 1 of 3 sentences correctly as dictated
3. **Speaking:** Evaluated throughout the interview conversation

The vocabulary is basic — practice with the USCIS reading and writing vocabulary lists
available free at uscis.gov/citizenship.

### Civics test
- USCIS asks up to 10 questions from the official list of 100 civics questions
- You must answer 6 correctly to pass
- If 65+ with 20+ years as LPR: 20-question list, must answer 6 of 10 correctly
- Study materials are free at uscis.gov/citizenship — flashcards, practice tests, videos

### If you fail
- You get a second chance: USCIS reschedules you within 60-90 days to retake
  the portion(s) you failed.
- If you fail both attempts, the application is denied. You can reapply.

## 4. Special Paths to Citizenship

### Military service
- Active duty or Selected Reserve members may be eligible after 1 year of service
- Served during designated periods of hostilities: may naturalize immediately
- No LPR time requirement in some cases
- Form N-400 filed with proof of military service

### Citizenship through parents (acquisition/derivation)
- Born abroad to a U.S. citizen parent: may already be a citizen — check INA §301, §309
- Derived citizenship: became a citizen automatically when parent naturalized
  (if you were under 18, an LPR, and in legal/physical custody of the citizen parent)
- File N-600 (Application for Certificate of Citizenship) to document acquired/derived citizenship

### Spouses of certain government/military employees
- Spouses of U.S. citizens stationed abroad may have modified requirements
- Check INA §319(b) for details

## 5. Common Issues and Disqualifiers

### Criminal history
Most serious concern for naturalization applicants. Key rules:

⚠️ **CRITICAL WARNING:** Applying for naturalization puts your ENTIRE immigration
status under review. If USCIS discovers issues during the N-400 process (criminal
history, fraud, immigration violations you didn't disclose), they can not only
deny your application but potentially initiate removal proceedings against you.
If you have ANY criminal history or immigration violations, consult an immigration
attorney BEFORE filing N-400.
- **Must disclose everything** — arrests, charges, convictions, even if dismissed/expunged.
  Failure to disclose is itself a basis for denial (or future denaturalization).
- **Aggravated felonies:** Permanent bar to good moral character.
- **Other crimes:** Evaluated case-by-case. Crimes involving moral turpitude (CIMT),
  drug offenses, fraud, and multiple convictions are problematic.
- **DUI/DWI:** One DUI may not bar you; multiple DUIs or a DUI with aggravating
  factors may indicate lack of good moral character.

⚠️ If you have ANY criminal history, consult an immigration attorney before filing N-400.
Applying for naturalization puts your entire immigration status under review —
a denied N-400 can in some cases trigger removal proceedings.

### Tax issues
- USCIS checks tax compliance. File all required returns before applying.
- Owing taxes is less problematic than not filing at all.
- IRS installment agreements show good faith.

### Selective Service
- Males who were required to register with Selective Service (ages 18-25)
  and didn't may have difficulty proving good moral character.
- If you're over 31, you can provide a Status Information Letter from SSS
  to explain the failure to register.

### Extended absences
- Any single trip of 6+ months is a red flag.
- Apply for N-470 (Preservation of Residence) BEFORE a planned extended absence.

## 6. After Naturalization

### What you can now do
- Vote in U.S. elections
- Obtain a U.S. passport
- Sponsor relatives for immigration (petition for parents, siblings — not available to LPRs)
- Travel without green card reentry concerns
- Hold certain government positions
- Protection from deportation (with very narrow exceptions)

### What to do immediately
- Register to vote (if you wish)
- Apply for a U.S. passport (Form DS-11 at a passport acceptance facility)
- Update Social Security records (bring your Certificate of Naturalization to SSA)
- Notify USCIS if you move within 90 days

### Dual citizenship
- The U.S. generally permits dual citizenship but doesn't formally recognize it.
- Check your other country's laws — some countries require you to renounce
  upon naturalizing elsewhere.
- Using another country's passport to enter that country is fine, but always
  use your U.S. passport to enter the U.S.
