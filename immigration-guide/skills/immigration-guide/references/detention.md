## 1. Understanding Immigration Detention

### What it is
Immigration detention is civil (not criminal) custody. ICE (Immigration and Customs
Enforcement) detains noncitizens during removal proceedings or pending deportation.
Detention can last from days to months or even years.

### Who gets detained
- People apprehended at or near the border
- People arrested by ICE during interior enforcement operations
- People with final removal orders awaiting deportation
- People with certain criminal convictions who are subject to mandatory detention
- People who overstayed visas or violated immigration status and came to ICE's attention

### Types of detention
- **ICE detention facilities** (privately or government-operated)
- **County jails** under contract with ICE
- **Family residential centers** (for families with children)
- **Alternatives to Detention (ATD)** — ankle monitors, check-ins, phone apps

### Mandatory detention
Certain individuals must be detained with no right to bond:
- Arriving aliens in expedited removal (unless they pass credible fear screening)
- Certain criminal aliens (aggravated felonies, controlled substance offenses,
  certain firearms offenses, multiple CIMTs)
- Suspected terrorists
- People subject to mandatory detention under INA §236(c)

⚠️ Whether someone is subject to mandatory detention is a legal determination.
An attorney can argue that mandatory detention doesn't apply.

## 2. Rights of Detained Individuals

### You have the right to:
- **Know why you're detained** — receive a Notice to Appear or other charging document
- **Contact your country's consulate** — this is a critical right under the Vienna
  Convention on Consular Relations. The detention facility MUST inform you of this
  right. Your consulate can provide assistance, connect you with legal resources,
  and monitor your treatment. Ask to contact your consulate immediately.
- **Contact an attorney** — detention facilities must provide access to legal calls
  and legal visits (though in practice, access varies)
- **A bond hearing** (unless subject to mandatory detention) before an immigration
  judge to request release
- **Due process in removal proceedings** — present evidence, have an attorney
  (at your own expense), appeal decisions
- **Medical care** — facilities are required to provide adequate medical care
- **Be free from abuse** — you have the right to report abuse, assault, or
  sexual violence without retaliation
- **File complaints** — about conditions, treatment, or violations
- **Religious practice** — reasonable accommodations for religious observance
- **Not be held indefinitely** — the Supreme Court has held that prolonged detention
  without adequate review raises due process concerns (Zadvydas v. Davis)

### You do NOT have:
- The right to a government-provided attorney (no public defender in immigration court)
- The right to bail as in criminal cases (bond is different — see section 3)
- The right to a phone call upon arrest (this is a criminal right, not immigration)
  — though facilities typically allow calls

## 3. Bond and Release Options

### Immigration bond
- Set by either ICE or an immigration judge
- Minimum $1,500 — typically $5,000 to $25,000+
- Must be paid in full (cash or approved surety bond)
- The bond is a guarantee that you'll appear at all future hearings
- If you attend all hearings, the bond is refunded at the end of your case
  (regardless of outcome)

### How to request bond

**From ICE (custody determination):**
- ICE makes an initial custody determination when you're first detained
- ICE can set bond, release you on your own recognizance, or deny release
- If ICE sets bond, you can pay it or request a bond hearing before an IJ

**From an immigration judge:**
- File a request for bond hearing (oral or written request to the immigration court)
- The judge considers:
  - Whether you're a flight risk
  - Whether you're a danger to the community
  - Your ties to the community (family, employment, property)
  - Your immigration history (compliance with prior orders, prior removals)
  - Your criminal history
- The judge can set bond, lower bond, or deny bond

### Preparing for a bond hearing
Gather and present:
- Evidence of ties to the community (family members, especially U.S. citizens/LPRs)
- Employment history and letters from employers
- Tax returns showing community contribution
- Letters of support from family, friends, religious leaders
- Evidence of lack of criminal history (or evidence of rehabilitation)
- Evidence that you'll appear at future hearings (prior compliance, stable address)
- If applicable: evidence of eligibility for immigration relief (pending applications)

### Other release options
- **Order of Supervision (OSUP):** ICE can release you with conditions (check-ins,
  GPS monitoring) — especially if deportation isn't immediately feasible
- **Parole from detention:** ICE can parole you for humanitarian reasons or
  because you're a witness in proceedings
- **Habeas corpus petition:** If detained for an unreasonable length of time,
  you can petition a federal court for release (see Zadvydas for post-removal-order
  detention exceeding 6 months)

### Paying bond
- Pay at any ICE/ERO field office (not at the detention facility)
- Must be paid by someone NOT in detention
- Bring the detainee's A-number (alien registration number) and full name
- Pay by cashier's check or money order payable to "Department of Homeland Security"
  (personal checks and cash may not be accepted — check with the local office)
- Bond companies can post surety bonds (typically charge 15-20% of bond amount as fee)

## 4. Finding a Detained Person

### ICE Online Detainee Locator
- Website: locator.ice.gov
- Search by name and country of birth, or A-number
- Shows the detention facility and contact information
- Note: may not be updated immediately — can take 24-72 hours after transfer

### If the locator doesn't work
- Call the ICE ERO (Enforcement and Removal Operations) detention reporting
  information line: 1-888-351-4024
- Contact the detained person's local ICE field office
- Contact an immigration attorney who can check EOIR (court) records
- Ask a congressional representative's office to make an inquiry

## 5. How to Help a Detained Family Member

### Immediate steps
1. **Find out where they are** (see section 4)
2. **Get them an attorney** — this is the most impactful thing you can do.
   Contact legal aid organizations (section 7) or hire a private attorney.
3. **Attend bond hearing (if applicable)** — your presence shows community ties
4. **Gather evidence for bond** — community ties, employment, family relationships
5. **Be prepared to pay bond** if it's granted
6. **Stay in contact** — detained individuals can receive calls and mail.
   Add money to their commissary account if possible.
7. **Document everything** — keep copies of all documents, receipts, and notices

### Communicating with a detained person
- Phone: Most facilities have phone systems where detainees can make collect calls
  or calls to pre-approved numbers. Services like Talton, ICSolutions, or GTL
  are commonly used. You may need to set up an account.
- Mail: You can send letters and some documents by mail. Check facility-specific
  rules for what's allowed.
- Visits: Most facilities allow in-person visits on a schedule. Check the facility's
  website or call for visiting hours and rules.
- Legal visits: Attorneys can visit outside regular visiting hours.

## 6. Detention Conditions and Complaints

### Standards
ICE detention facilities are supposed to meet standards set by ICE's Performance-Based
National Detention Standards (PBNDS). These cover medical care, food, recreation,
access to legal materials, grievance procedures, and protection from harm.

### How to file complaints
- **Internal grievance:** Each facility has a grievance process. File written complaints.
- **ICE Office of Professional Responsibility (OPR):** For complaints about ICE
  employee conduct.
- **DHS Office of Inspector General (OIG):** For serious complaints about facility
  conditions, abuse, or misconduct. Hotline: 1-800-323-8603
- **DHS Office for Civil Rights and Civil Liberties (CRCL):** For civil rights violations.
  Email: CRCLCompliance@hq.dhs.gov
- **Congressional representatives:** Contact your representative's or senator's
  office — they have constituent services staff who can intervene.

### Medical care
- You have the right to medical care in detention, including emergency care,
  chronic care, mental health services, and dental care.
- If medical needs aren't being met: file a grievance, have family contact your
  attorney, and contact advocacy organizations.

## 7. Legal Resources for Detained Individuals

### Free legal services
- **CLINIC (Catholic Legal Immigration Network):** cliniclegal.org — directory of
  legal service providers near detention facilities
- **Immigration Advocates Network Legal Directory:** immigrationadvocates.org/legaldirectory
- **ABA's Immigration Justice Project:** Free and low-cost representation referrals
- **Legal aid hotlines:** Many states have immigration legal aid hotlines
- **Law school immigration clinics:** Many law schools have clinics that serve
  detained individuals

### Know Your Rights (KYR) information
Many organizations conduct Know Your Rights presentations at detention facilities.
If you're detained, ask facility staff about upcoming KYR presentations.

### Funding bond
If you can't afford bond:
- **National Bond Fund / community bond funds:** Some organizations help pay
  immigration bonds. Search for immigration bond funds in your area.
- **The National Immigrant Justice Center** and similar organizations may
  have bond fund programs.
- Churches, mosques, synagogues, and community organizations sometimes help
  with bond.

### If you can't find an attorney
- Represent yourself (pro se) — you have the right to do this, though it's
  very difficult. Ask the immigration court for a list of free legal service
  providers in the area.
- File a motion for a continuance (delay) to find an attorney.
- Contact your consulate — they may be able to connect you with legal resources.
