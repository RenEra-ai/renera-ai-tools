## 1. Temporary Protected Status (TPS)

### What it is
Temporary protection from removal for nationals of designated countries experiencing
armed conflict, natural disasters, or other extraordinary conditions. TPS provides
lawful status and work authorization but does NOT provide a path to permanent residence
on its own.

### Currently designated countries
⚠️ This list changes frequently — verify the current list at uscis.gov/tps before
citing it to a user. Do not rely on this file for current designation status.
As of early 2025, designated countries included: Afghanistan, Burma (Myanmar),
Cameroon, El Salvador, Ethiopia, Haiti, Honduras, Lebanon, Nepal, Nicaragua,
Somalia, South Sudan, Sudan, Syria, Ukraine, Venezuela, Yemen, and others.

### Eligibility requirements
- National of a designated country (or stateless person who last habitually resided there)
- Physically present in the U.S. on the designation date (or re-designation date)
- Continuously resided in the U.S. since the specified date
- Filed during the open registration (or re-registration) period
- Not barred by criminal history or other inadmissibility grounds
- Pass background checks

### How to apply
- **Form I-821** (Application for TPS) — filed during the registration period
- **Form I-765** (Application for Employment Authorization) — filed with or after I-821
- **Filing fee:** ⚠️ Check current fees at uscis.gov — do not rely on this file
- Must re-register during each extension period to maintain status

### Key points for applicants
- **Initial registration windows are critical.** If you miss the window, you may not
  be able to register later. Set calendar alerts for Federal Register notices.
- **Re-registration:** When TPS is extended, you must re-register during the window
  (typically 60 days). File I-821 AND I-765 again.
- **Travel:** TPS holders can apply for advance parole (I-131) to travel abroad.
  Returning with advance parole may create a lawful "admission" that could enable
  adjustment of status through a family petition — consult an attorney about this.
- **EAD:** Your work permit is tied to your TPS status. Automatic extensions exist
  through multiple pathways (FRN notices and H.R. 1 pending-renewal rules).
  ⚠️ For any "until when is my EAD valid" question, use /imm-ead-tps-check —
  the answer depends on which extension path applies and cannot be read from this file.

### Common pitfalls
- Missing re-registration deadline = loss of TPS status
- Not understanding that TPS doesn't lead to a green card by itself
- Criminal conduct during TPS can result in loss of status and removal
- Traveling without advance parole while on TPS = abandonment of status

## 2. Uniting for Ukraine (U4U) Parole

### What it is
A parole program for Ukrainian citizens and their immediate family members
displaced by the Russian invasion. Provides temporary authorization to enter the
U.S. and receive work authorization.

### Eligibility (original program)
- Ukrainian citizen (or immediate family member of a Ukrainian citizen)
- Resided in Ukraine as of February 11, 2022
- Have a U.S.-based sponsor (individual or organization) who files Form I-134A
  (Declaration of Financial Support)
- Pass vetting and background checks
- Not otherwise inadmissible

### How it works
1. U.S.-based sponsor submits I-134A through myUSCIS
2. USCIS vets the sponsor and confirms financial support
3. Beneficiary completes processing (biometrics, medical exam, vetting)
4. Beneficiary authorized to travel to the U.S.
5. Paroled into the U.S. for up to 2 years
6. Apply for EAD (Form I-765, category (c)(11)) upon arrival

### Current status and extensions
⚠️ The status of U4U re-parole and extensions changes by executive action.
CHECK CURRENT USCIS GUIDANCE — do not rely on this file for current availability.
Fetch uscis.gov/humanitarian/uniting-for-ukraine for current program status.

### Path forward for U4U parolees
- TPS for Ukraine (if designated and you were present during the designation period)
- Asylum (if you have a persecution-based claim)
- Family-based petitions (if you have a qualifying U.S. citizen or LPR relative)
- Employment-based sponsorship
- Humanitarian parole renewal (if available — verify current availability)

## 3. Humanitarian Parole (General)

### What it is
Case-by-case authorization to enter the U.S. temporarily for urgent humanitarian
reasons or significant public benefit. Not a visa — a discretionary grant.

### How to apply
- **Form I-131** (Application for Travel Document) — filed by the person seeking
  parole or by someone on their behalf
- Must demonstrate urgent humanitarian reasons or significant public benefit
- Filing fee required (⚠️ check current amount at uscis.gov; fee waiver may be available)

### Key facts
- Granted for a specific time period (varies by case)
- Does NOT provide a path to permanent residence on its own
- Can apply for EAD (Form I-765) once paroled into the U.S.
- Parole can be terminated at any time

### Examples of humanitarian reasons
- Serious medical emergency requiring treatment in the U.S.
- Organ donation
- Family reunification in urgent circumstances (e.g., a child's parent is dying)
- Fleeing immediate danger

## 4. CHNV Parole Programs (Cuba, Haiti, Nicaragua, Venezuela)

### What they are
Country-specific parole programs for nationals of Cuba, Haiti, Nicaragua, and Venezuela.
Allow eligible individuals to enter the U.S. for up to 2 years with work authorization.

### Key facts
- Require a U.S.-based sponsor who files Form I-134A
- Beneficiaries must be outside the U.S. at time of application
- Background checks and vetting required
- Work authorization included
- Limited number of authorizations processed monthly

### Current status
⚠️ These programs have been subject to legal challenges and policy changes.
⚠️ Verify current availability at uscis.gov before beginning the process —
do not rely on this file for current availability or program status.

## 5. Parole-in-Place for Military Families

### What it is
Parole for certain family members (spouses, children, parents) of active-duty
U.S. military members, veterans, and reservists who are already in the U.S.
without lawful immigration status.

### Significance
Being paroled-in-place creates a lawful "admission" — which is critical because
adjustment of status (I-485) generally requires a lawful admission. Without this,
someone who entered without inspection cannot normally adjust status even if they
have an approved I-130 from a U.S. citizen spouse.

### Eligibility
- Must be the spouse, parent, or stepchild of an active-duty service member, veteran,
  or reservist
- Must be physically present in the U.S.
- Must not have a disqualifying criminal history

### How to apply
⚠️ Check uscis.gov for the current application process — this program has been
implemented through various mechanisms and its status may change by executive action.

## 6. Deferred Enforced Departure (DED)

### What it is
Presidential designation providing temporary protection from removal for nationals
of certain countries. Similar to TPS but authorized by executive order rather
than statute.

### Key differences from TPS
- DED is an executive action, not a statutory designation
- Generally provides work authorization
- No formal registration process like TPS
- Can be extended or terminated by the President

### Currently designated countries
⚠️ Check Federal Register notices for current DED designations — do not rely
on this file for current designation status.

## 7. DACA (Deferred Action for Childhood Arrivals)

⚠️ **EXTREME VOLATILITY WARNING**: DACA has been subject to extensive litigation,
multiple court orders, and policy changes since its creation in 2012. Program
availability for initial applications vs. renewals may differ at any given time.
**Always verify current program status at uscis.gov/DACA before advising anyone.**

### What it is
A deferred action program for individuals who were brought to the United States as
children. DACA provides temporary protection from removal and work authorization, but
does NOT provide lawful immigration status and does NOT provide a path to permanent
residence on its own.

### Eligibility
- Arrived in the U.S. before the applicant's 16th birthday
- Continuous residence in the U.S. since June 15, 2007
- Physically present in the U.S. on June 15, 2012
- Under 31 years of age on June 15, 2012
- Had no lawful immigration status on June 15, 2012
- Currently in school, graduated from high school, obtained a GED, or are an
  honorably discharged veteran of the U.S. Armed Forces
- No significant criminal history (no felony, significant misdemeanor, or three
  or more non-significant misdemeanors; not a threat to national security or
  public safety)

### How to apply
- **Form I-821D** (Consideration of Deferred Action for Childhood Arrivals)
- **Form I-765** (Application for Employment Authorization)
- **Form I-765WS** (Worksheet)
- All three forms filed together as a package
- Filing fee required (⚠️ check current amount at uscis.gov)

### Work authorization
- EAD issued under category (c)(33)
- 2-year grants (when available)
- Must be renewed before expiration — plan ahead, processing takes time

### Renewal
- Must file renewal request before current DACA and EAD expire
- Recommended to file 120–150 days before expiration
- Same forms as initial request (I-821D, I-765, I-765WS)

### Travel
- Advance parole has been available during some periods for DACA recipients
  (Form I-131, for humanitarian, educational, or employment purposes)
- Returning with advance parole may create a lawful admission that could be
  useful for adjustment of status through a family-based petition — consult
  an attorney about this strategy
- ⚠️ Advance parole availability for DACA recipients changes with policy — verify
  before applying

### Common pitfalls
- DACA does NOT provide a path to permanent residence on its own
- DACA does NOT confer lawful status — it is prosecutorial discretion
- Letting DACA expire before filing renewal can cause gaps in work authorization
  and deferred action
- Criminal conduct can result in DACA termination and referral to ICE
- Traveling without advance parole while on DACA = leaving the U.S. without
  authorization and potentially being unable to return

## 8. T Visa (Trafficking Victims)

### What it is
A nonimmigrant visa for victims of severe forms of human trafficking. Provides
immigration status, work authorization, and a path to permanent residence.

### Eligibility
- Victim of a severe form of trafficking in persons (sex trafficking by force,
  fraud, or coercion, or trafficking of a minor for sex; labor trafficking by
  force, fraud, or coercion)
- Physically present in the U.S., American Samoa, the CNMI, or at a port of entry
  on account of the trafficking
- Has complied with any reasonable request for assistance in the investigation or
  prosecution of the trafficking (or is under 18 years old, or is unable to
  cooperate due to physical or psychological trauma)
- Would suffer extreme hardship involving unusual and severe harm if removed from
  the U.S.

### How to apply
- **Form I-914** (Application for T Nonimmigrant Status)
- **Form I-914 Supplement B** (Declaration of Law Enforcement Officer for Victim
  of Trafficking in Persons) — provides law enforcement certification
  - Not always required: applicants under 18 and those who can demonstrate
    trauma-based inability to cooperate may be exempt
- Filing fee: none (no fee for I-914)

### Derivative beneficiaries
- Spouse and children of the principal applicant
- Parents and unmarried siblings under 18 (if principal applicant is under 21)
- Filed on Form I-914 Supplement A

### Work authorization
- Automatic upon T visa approval — no separate EAD application needed
- May also be eligible for pre-certification employment authorization in some cases

### Annual cap
- 5,000 T-1 (principal) visas per fiscal year
- Derivatives do NOT count against the cap

### Path to green card
- Can apply for adjustment of status after 3 years in T nonimmigrant status, OR
  when the investigation or prosecution is complete (whichever comes first)
- Must demonstrate continuous physical presence in the U.S.
- Must demonstrate good moral character
- Must show compliance with reasonable law enforcement requests (or qualify for exception)
- Filed on Form I-485

### Key points
- Confidentiality protections: USCIS cannot disclose information about T visa
  applicants to the trafficker or anyone else not authorized
- Can apply even if undocumented at the time of application
- Does NOT require a criminal conviction of the trafficker
- Applicants may be eligible for federal benefits as certified trafficking victims

### Common pitfalls
- Not understanding that law enforcement certification is helpful but not always
  required — lack of certification does not automatically bar an application
- Failing to document the trafficking and its effects thoroughly
- Not applying for derivative family members who may also be at risk
- Waiting too long to apply — evidence and memories can fade

## 9. U Visa (Crime Victims)

### What it is
A nonimmigrant visa for victims of qualifying crimes who have suffered substantial
physical or mental abuse and who assist law enforcement in the investigation or
prosecution of the crime.

### Qualifying crimes
Domestic violence, sexual assault, trafficking, kidnapping, abduction, torture,
rape, incest, female genital mutilation, being held hostage, involuntary servitude,
slave trade, peonage, fraud in foreign labor contracting, blackmail, extortion,
manslaughter, murder, felonious assault, witness tampering, obstruction of justice,
perjury, solicitation to commit any of these, and attempt, conspiracy, or solicitation
to commit any of the above. The full statutory list is at 8 U.S.C. § 1101(a)(15)(U)(iii).

### Eligibility
- Victim of a qualifying crime (or qualifying criminal activity)
- Suffered substantial physical or mental abuse as a result of the crime
- Possesses information about the criminal activity
- Has obtained a law enforcement certification (I-918 Supplement B) from a
  certifying agency confirming the applicant was helpful, is being helpful, or
  is likely to be helpful in the investigation or prosecution
- The qualifying crime occurred in the U.S. or violated U.S. law
- Admissible to the U.S. (or eligible for a waiver of inadmissibility — the U visa
  waiver under INA § 212(d)(14) is broad)

### How to apply
- **Form I-918** (Petition for U Nonimmigrant Status)
- **Form I-918 Supplement B** (U Nonimmigrant Status Certification) — signed by
  a certifying law enforcement agency (police, prosecutors, judges, federal/state
  agencies, child protective services, EEOC, DOL, and others)
- **Form I-192** (Application for Advance Permission to Enter as a Nonimmigrant)
  — if a waiver of inadmissibility is needed
- Filing fee: none (no fee for I-918)

### Annual cap and backlog
- 10,000 U-1 (principal) visas per fiscal year
- There is a significant backlog — petitioners may wait years for a visa number
  to become available

### Bona fide determination
- While waiting in the queue, USCIS may issue a "bona fide determination" (BFD)
  for petitions that appear to meet eligibility requirements
- BFD recipients receive deferred action status and work authorization (EAD)
  while waiting for a U visa number

### Derivative beneficiaries
- Spouse and children of the principal applicant
- Parents and unmarried siblings under 18 (if principal applicant is under 21)
- Unmarried siblings under 16 qualify for additional derivative categories

### Work authorization
- Granted automatically with U visa approval
- May be granted earlier with a bona fide determination

### Path to green card
- Can apply for adjustment of status after 3 years of continuous physical presence
  in the U.S. in U nonimmigrant status
- Must not have unreasonably refused to assist in the investigation or prosecution
- Must demonstrate continuous physical presence
- Must show that adjustment is justified on humanitarian, family unity, or public
  interest grounds
- Filed on Form I-485

### Key points
- The certifying law enforcement agency is NOT the one that decides U visa eligibility —
  the agency only certifies helpfulness. USCIS makes the final eligibility determination.
- The law enforcement certification must be signed by the head of the certifying agency
  or a designated official
- Multiple certifying agencies can provide certifications for the same crime
- Indirect victims (bystanders who witnessed the crime, or family members of
  deceased victims) may also qualify

### Common pitfalls
- The backlog means years of waiting — manage client expectations and ensure they
  understand the timeline
- Law enforcement certifications have a signing deadline (generally must not be
  older than 6 months at time of filing — ⚠️ verify current rule)
- Withdrawal of certification by the agency does not automatically result in denial
  of the U visa petition — USCIS evaluates the full record
- Not understanding that the waiver of inadmissibility for U visa applicants is
  among the most generous in immigration law — many grounds are waivable
- Failing to apply for derivative family members who may need protection

## 10. VAWA Self-Petition

⚠️ **SAFETY NOTICE**: If the person seeking assistance is in immediate danger, provide
the National Domestic Violence Hotline: **1-800-799-7233** (1-800-799-SAFE). Safety
planning must come before immigration strategy.

### What it is
The Violence Against Women Act (VAWA) allows certain abused spouses, children, and
parents of U.S. citizens or lawful permanent residents to self-petition for immigration
benefits without the abuser's knowledge or cooperation.

### Who qualifies
- **Abused spouse** of a U.S. citizen or lawful permanent resident
- **Abused child** (unmarried, under 21) of a U.S. citizen or lawful permanent resident
- **Abused parent** of an adult (21+) U.S. citizen

### How to apply
- **Form I-360** (Petition for Amerasian, Widow(er), or Special Immigrant) —
  self-petition filed by the victim
- Filing fee: none (no fee for VAWA self-petitions)
- Can also file concurrently with I-485 if otherwise eligible for adjustment

### Key feature: confidentiality
- The abuser is NEVER notified of the self-petition — full confidentiality
  protections under 8 U.S.C. § 1367
- USCIS cannot contact the abuser, and the abuser cannot access USCIS records
  related to the self-petition
- Information about the self-petition cannot be disclosed to third parties

### Work authorization
- Can apply for EAD once I-360 is approved
- May also receive work authorization once a prima facie case is established
  (before full adjudication)

### Path to green card
- VAWA self-petitioners can adjust status (I-485) if otherwise eligible
- If the abuser is a U.S. citizen: immediate relative category (no visa backlog)
- If the abuser is an LPR: preference category (may have visa backlog)
- Self-petitioners are eligible for the INA § 212(h) and (i) waivers of
  inadmissibility

### Battery or extreme cruelty
This includes but is not limited to:
- Physical violence (hitting, slapping, kicking, choking)
- Threats of physical violence or harm
- Emotional and psychological abuse (intimidation, degradation, isolation)
- Economic abuse (withholding money, controlling finances, threatening destitution)
- Sexual abuse
- Immigration-related abuse (threatening to withdraw petition, threatening
  deportation, hiding or destroying immigration documents)

### Requirements
- Good moral character (required for the self-petitioner)
- Marriage must have been entered into in good faith (for spousal claims — not
  solely for immigration benefits)
- Resided with the abuser (at some point)
- Battery or extreme cruelty occurred during the qualifying relationship
- The self-petitioner or their child was abused

### Evidence
No single type of evidence is required. USCIS considers the totality of the
evidence, which may include:
- Police reports and incident reports
- Protection/restraining orders
- Medical records documenting injuries
- Photographs of injuries or property damage
- Affidavits from witnesses (friends, family, neighbors, coworkers)
- School records showing impact on children
- Counseling or therapy records
- Records from shelters or domestic violence organizations
- The self-petitioner's own detailed personal declaration

### Common pitfalls
- Believing that without a police report or criminal conviction, a VAWA case
  cannot succeed — this is false; no single type of evidence is required
- Not understanding that the abuser does NOT need to be convicted or even charged
  with a crime
- Failing to include a thorough personal declaration describing the abuse
- Not applying for derivative children who may also need protection
- Not seeking safety planning assistance in addition to immigration relief

## 11. Special Immigrant Juvenile Status (SIJS)

### What it is
An immigration protection for children in the United States who have been abused,
neglected, or abandoned by one or both parents. SIJS provides a path to lawful
permanent residence (green card).

### Eligibility
- Under 21 years of age
- Unmarried
- Physically present in the United States
- Have a state court order with the required findings (see below)

### Required state court findings
The state court order must find ALL of the following:
1. The child is dependent on a juvenile court, or has been legally committed to
   or placed under the custody of a state agency, department, individual, or entity
2. Reunification with one or both parents is not viable due to abuse, neglect,
   abandonment, or a similar basis under state law
3. It is not in the child's best interest to be returned to their (or their
   parent's) country of nationality or last habitual residence

### Two-step process
1. **State court order:** Obtain the required state court findings through a
   state juvenile, family, or probate court proceeding (the specific court varies
   by state)
2. **USCIS petition:** File Form I-360 with USCIS, attaching the state court
   order as evidence

### How to apply
- **Form I-360** (Petition for Amerasian, Widow(er), or Special Immigrant) —
  filed with the state court order
- Filing fee: none (no fee for SIJS-based I-360 petitions)
- USCIS consent function: USCIS must provide consent to the grant of SIJ
  classification before or at the time of adjudication — this is a separate
  determination from the state court order

### Path to green card
- After I-360 approval, file Form I-485 for adjustment of status
- SIJS falls under the EB-4 (employment-based fourth preference) category for
  visa number purposes

### Aging out: critical issue
- The child must be under 21 and unmarried at multiple stages of the process:
  at the time of the state court order, at the time of I-360 filing, and at
  the time of adjudication
- Delays in either state court proceedings or USCIS processing can cause the
  child to age out (turn 21) before the case is complete
- Some statutory protections against aging out exist, but this remains a
  high-risk area — prioritize these cases and file as early as possible
- Marriage at any point before the process is complete will disqualify the applicant

### Annual cap and backlog
- SIJS visa numbers are allocated under the EB-4 category
- Per-country limits may create backlogs for applicants from certain countries
  (notably El Salvador, Guatemala, Honduras, Mexico, and India at various times)
- ⚠️ Visa bulletin wait times change — check the current visa bulletin at
  travel.state.gov for the most up-to-date priority dates

### Common pitfalls
- State court standards vary significantly by state — what qualifies as "abuse,
  neglect, or abandonment" differs by jurisdiction
- The state court order is NOT itself an immigration benefit — it is a prerequisite
  for the USCIS petition. The state court does not grant immigration status.
- USCIS consent function requirement: USCIS independently determines whether SIJ
  classification is warranted, even after a state court issues the required findings
- Not filing the I-360 promptly after obtaining the state court order — delays
  increase aging-out risk
- Failure to monitor visa bulletin priority dates — the child may need to wait
  for a visa number before adjusting status
- Not understanding that the child must remain unmarried throughout the entire
  process

## 12. Transitioning from Humanitarian Status to Permanent Residence

### From humanitarian status to green card
Humanitarian programs (TPS, parole, DED) do NOT automatically lead to permanent
residence. However, they can be combined with other immigration pathways:

### TPS holders
- If you have a U.S. citizen or LPR relative who can file I-130 for you:
  - Travel with advance parole (I-131) and return — this may create a lawful admission
  - Then file I-485 based on the approved I-130
  - ⚠️ This strategy has been upheld by most courts but consult an attorney — it
    is a complex legal strategy that depends on circuit court precedent
- Employment-based sponsorship is also possible while on TPS

### Parolees (U4U, CHNV, humanitarian parole)
- Parole generally counts as a lawful admission for adjustment of status purposes
- If you have an approved immigrant petition (I-130 from spouse, or I-140), you
  may be able to adjust status
- Apply for TPS if your country is designated and you meet the eligibility dates
- Asylum may be available if you have a persecution-based claim
- Key issue: parole is time-limited. Plan your next step BEFORE your parole expires.

### Critical timing
- Don't let your humanitarian status expire without a plan
- Filing an I-485 (adjustment) while in valid humanitarian status generally protects
  you from removal while the application is pending
- If your humanitarian status expires and you have no pending application,
  you become undocumented and potentially removable
