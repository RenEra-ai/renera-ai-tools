## 1. Affirmative Asylum (Filing with USCIS)

### Eligibility
You may apply for asylum if you have suffered persecution or have a well-founded
fear of future persecution based on one of five protected grounds:
1. **Race**
2. **Religion**
3. **Nationality**
4. **Political opinion**
5. **Membership in a particular social group** (PSG)

### Filing basics
- **Form:** I-589, Application for Asylum and for Withholding of Removal
- **Filing fee:** None (asylum applications are free)
- **Deadline:** Must file within 1 year of your last arrival in the U.S.
  - Exceptions exist for changed circumstances or extraordinary circumstances
  - The 1-year bar is strictly enforced — don't miss it
- **Where to file:** USCIS Service Center (currently the Nebraska Service Center
  for most applicants — check I-589 instructions for current address)

### What to prepare
- Completed I-589 with a detailed, specific personal declaration
- Your declaration (the most important document): chronological narrative of
  persecution or feared persecution, identifying WHO persecuted you, WHAT happened,
  WHEN, WHERE, and WHY (tied to a protected ground)
- Country condition evidence: State Department human rights reports, news articles,
  reports from Human Rights Watch, Amnesty International, or country-specific experts
- Corroborating evidence: medical records, police reports, photos, affidavits from
  witnesses, threatening messages, identity documents
- Two passport-style photos

### The affirmative process
1. File I-589 with USCIS
2. Receive a receipt notice (confirmation of filing)
3. Biometrics appointment
4. Asylum interview at a USCIS Asylum Office
   - Conducted by an asylum officer (not a judge)
   - You may bring an attorney but the attorney's role is limited
   - You will testify about your claim — be prepared to describe your experiences
5. Decision: grant, refer to immigration court, or deny
   - If referred to court, your case becomes "defensive" (see below)

### Interview preparation tips
- Practice telling your story chronologically and consistently.
- Be specific about dates, places, names of persecutors.
- If you don't remember something, say so — don't guess.
- Bring an interpreter if you're not fluent in English (USCIS provides one, but
  you can bring your own as well).
- Arrive early with all original documents.

## 2. Defensive Asylum (Immigration Court)

### When this applies
- USCIS refers your affirmative case to court
- You're placed in removal proceedings and raise asylum as a defense
- You're apprehended at the border and pass a credible fear screening

### Key differences from affirmative
- Heard by an Immigration Judge (IJ), not an asylum officer
- Government is represented by a DHS trial attorney who may cross-examine you
- More adversarial, formal proceedings
- You have the right to an attorney BUT the government does not provide one
  (no right to a public defender in immigration court)

### Process
1. Master calendar hearing (scheduling, identify issues)
2. Individual (merits) hearing — you testify, present evidence, witnesses
3. Judge issues decision (sometimes same day, sometimes later)
4. If denied, you may appeal to the Board of Immigration Appeals (BIA)

## 3. Withholding of Removal (INA §241(b)(3))

### What it is
A higher standard than asylum but with important differences:
- Must prove persecution is "more likely than not" (higher than asylum's "well-founded fear")
- No 1-year filing deadline
- Does NOT provide a path to a green card
- Cannot be used to bring family members
- Only prevents removal to the specific country of persecution

### When to pursue
- You missed the 1-year asylum filing deadline
- You have a bar to asylum (certain criminal convictions, firm resettlement)
  but still face persecution

## 4. Convention Against Torture (CAT) Protection

### What it is
Protection from removal to a country where you would "more likely than not"
be tortured by or with the acquiescence of a government official.

### Key facts
- No filing deadline
- No requirement to show persecution based on a protected ground
- Available even to people with serious criminal convictions
- Two forms: withholding of removal under CAT, or deferral of removal under CAT
- Deferral can be terminated if conditions change; withholding is more permanent

## 5. Credible Fear and Expedited Removal

### What it is
If you're apprehended at or near the border (or arrive without valid documents),
you may be placed in expedited removal. You can request asylum by expressing
a fear of return — this triggers a "credible fear" screening.

### Process
1. Express fear of return to the immigration officer
2. Referred to an asylum officer for a credible fear interview (CFI)
3. Asylum officer determines if you have a "significant possibility" of establishing
   eligibility for asylum
4. If you pass: placed in regular removal proceedings where you can pursue asylum
5. If you fail: you can request review by an immigration judge (within 7 days)
6. If the IJ upholds the negative finding: removal order executed

### Critical advice
- **Ask for asylum/express fear at the earliest opportunity.** Don't wait.
- You have the right to consult with someone before your CFI (but timing is tight).
- Be as specific and detailed as possible during the CFI.
- The standard is low — "significant possibility" — but you must articulate
  a connection to a protected ground.

## 6. Removal Proceedings Overview

### How proceedings start
- Notice to Appear (NTA) — the charging document. It lists the allegations
  and charges of removability against you.
- Check the NTA carefully: is your name correct? Date of entry? Visa status?
  Errors can be used in your defense.

### Your rights in removal proceedings
- Right to an attorney (at your own expense)
- Right to examine and present evidence
- Right to cross-examine government witnesses
- Right to appeal an IJ's decision to the BIA
- Right to an interpreter

### Forms of relief you may be eligible for
- Asylum, Withholding, CAT (see above)
- Cancellation of removal (10 years continuous presence + exceptional hardship
  to a U.S. citizen or LPR family member)
- Adjustment of status (if eligible through family or employment)
- Voluntary departure (leave on your own terms instead of a removal order)
- Prosecutorial discretion / administrative closure (case-by-case)

## 7. Relief from Removal

### Cancellation of removal for non-LPRs (INA §240A(b))
- 10 years continuous physical presence in the U.S.
- Good moral character during that period
- Removal would cause "exceptional and extremely unusual hardship" to a
  qualifying relative (U.S. citizen or LPR spouse, parent, or child)
- This is an extremely high standard — general hardship is not enough

### Cancellation for LPRs (INA §240A(a))
- 7 years continuous residence after admission
- 5 years as an LPR
- No aggravated felony conviction

### Voluntary departure
- Leave the U.S. at your own expense by a set date
- Avoids a formal removal order (which triggers bars to reentry)
- Must post a bond (typically $500+)
- If you don't depart by the deadline: automatic removal order + additional bars

## 8. Appeals

### Board of Immigration Appeals (BIA)
- File Notice of Appeal (Form EOIR-26) within 30 days of the IJ's decision
- BIA reviews the record — does not hear new testimony
- BIA can affirm, reverse, or remand to the IJ

### Federal court review
- Petition for review to the appropriate U.S. Circuit Court of Appeals
- Must file within 30 days of the BIA decision
- Court reviews legal questions — very deferential to factual findings
