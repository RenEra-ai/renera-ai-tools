## 1. Overview

### Two main paths
- **U.S. citizen spouse:** Immediate relative — no visa number wait, no quota.
  Fastest family-based path.
- **LPR (green card holder) spouse:** Family preference category 2A — subject to
  visa availability and potential wait times.

### Key concept: "admission" requirement
To adjust status (get a green card inside the U.S.), you generally must have been
"inspected and admitted or paroled" into the U.S. This means:

- **Entered with a visa or at a port of entry:** Can generally adjust status with
  a U.S. citizen spouse, even if you overstayed (INA §245(a) is forgiving for
  immediate relatives of U.S. citizens).
- **Entered without inspection (EWI):** Generally CANNOT adjust status inside the U.S.,
  even with a U.S. citizen spouse. Must consular process abroad — BUT this triggers
  the 3-year or 10-year unlawful presence bars. May need a waiver (I-601A).

⚠️ The EWI + unlawful presence situation is one of the most legally complex
scenarios in immigration. Strongly recommend an attorney.

## 2. Adjustment of Status (Form I-485) — Inside the U.S.

⚠️ **DO NOT LEAVE THE U.S.** before filing I-485 and receiving advance parole
(I-131 or combo card). Departing while out of status (e.g., overstayed visa)
triggers unlawful presence bars: 3 years (for 180 days–1 year overstay) or
10 years (for 1+ year). The immediate relative protection only works if you
stay in the U.S. and adjust from inside.

### When this path works
- You're in the U.S. with a lawful admission (entered with a visa, parole, etc.)
- Your spouse is a U.S. citizen (immediate relative — most flexible)
- Or your spouse is an LPR and your priority date is current

### What to file (typically filed concurrently)
1. **Form I-130** (Petition for Alien Relative) — filed by the U.S. citizen/LPR spouse
2. **Form I-485** (Application to Adjust Status) — filed by the immigrant spouse
3. **Form I-765** (EAD application) — for work authorization while waiting
4. **Form I-131** (Advance Parole) — for travel authorization while waiting
5. **Form I-864** (Affidavit of Support) — the petitioning spouse proves financial
   ability to support you at 125% of federal poverty guidelines

### Required documents checklist
- Completed forms (see above)
- Marriage certificate (certified copy)
- Evidence of U.S. citizen or LPR status of petitioning spouse
- Birth certificates for both spouses
- Passport and I-94 records for the immigrant spouse
- Two passport-style photos (for each form that requires them)
- Filing fees (check uscis.gov — filing I-485 includes EAD and advance parole fees)
- Medical examination report (Form I-693) from a USCIS-designated civil surgeon
- Evidence of bona fide marriage (see section 6)
- Financial evidence for I-864 (tax returns, W-2s, pay stubs, employment letter)
- Police clearance certificates or court records if any criminal history

### The interview
- USCIS will schedule a joint interview at your local field office
- Both spouses attend together
- Officer will ask about your relationship, how you met, your life together
- Officer reviews documents and may ask for additional evidence
- Be honest, consistent, and specific

### Timeline
- Immediate relatives (U.S. citizen spouse): typically 10-24 months from filing to green card
- LPR spouses: depends on visa bulletin — can be faster or much slower

## 3. Consular Processing — From Abroad

### When to use this path
- The immigrant spouse is outside the U.S.
- Or the immigrant spouse is in the U.S. but cannot adjust status (e.g., EWI)

### Process
1. U.S. citizen/LPR spouse files I-130 with USCIS
2. After I-130 approval, case transfers to the National Visa Center (NVC)
3. NVC collects fees, Form DS-260 (immigrant visa application), civil documents,
   and I-864 (Affidavit of Support)
4. NVC schedules an interview at the U.S. consulate/embassy in the immigrant
   spouse's home country
5. Interview at the consulate
6. If approved: immigrant visa issued, travel to U.S., admitted as LPR at the port of entry

### Unlawful presence bars
If the immigrant spouse was unlawfully present in the U.S. for:
- **180 days to 1 year**, then departed: 3-year bar from reentry
- **1 year or more**, then departed: 10-year bar from reentry

The I-601A provisional unlawful presence waiver can waive these bars IF:
- You're the spouse, parent, or child of a U.S. citizen or LPR
- You can show your qualifying relative would suffer "extreme hardship" if you're barred

⚠️ The waiver is complex and requires strong evidence. Get legal help.

## 4. K-1 Fiancé Visa

### What it is
Visa for the fiancé(e) of a U.S. citizen to enter the U.S. and marry within 90 days.

### Process
1. U.S. citizen files Form I-129F (Petition for Alien Fiancé)
2. After approval, case goes to the consulate
3. Fiancé applies for K-1 visa at the consulate, attends interview
4. If approved: enters the U.S. on K-1 visa
5. Must marry within 90 days of entry
6. After marriage: file I-485 to adjust to permanent residence

### Key facts
- Only available if the petitioner is a U.S. citizen (not LPR)
- Must have met in person within the past 2 years (with limited exceptions)
- K-1 holder cannot work until EAD is issued (applied for with I-485)
- K-1 holder should NOT leave the U.S. before filing I-485 and getting advance parole

### K-1 vs. CR-1/IR-1 (spouse visa)
| Factor | K-1 (Fiancé) | CR-1/IR-1 (Spouse) |
|---|---|---|
| File when | Before marriage | After marriage |
| Who files | U.S. citizen files I-129F | U.S. citizen files I-130 |
| Green card upon entry? | No — must adjust after entry | Yes — LPR status on arrival |
| Can work immediately? | No (need EAD) | Yes (has green card) |
| Overall timeline | Often faster to enter U.S. | Often faster to get green card |
| Total cost | Usually higher | Usually lower |

## 5. Conditional Residence and Removing Conditions

### Conditional green card (2-year card)
If your marriage was less than 2 years old when you became an LPR, you receive
a conditional (2-year) green card instead of a standard 10-year card.

### Removing conditions (Form I-751)
- **File jointly** with your spouse within the 90-day window before your conditional
  green card expires. Not before, not after.
- Provide updated evidence of bona fide marriage
- If approved: receive a 10-year green card

### If the marriage has ended (divorce, abuse, death)
You can file I-751 as a waiver of the joint filing requirement if:
- Your spouse died
- You divorced (must still show the marriage was genuine)
- You suffered battery or extreme cruelty by your U.S. citizen/LPR spouse
- Removal would cause extreme hardship

⚠️ If you're in an abusive marriage, you have options:
- VAWA (Violence Against Women Act) self-petition — you can file I-360 on your own
  without your spouse's knowledge or cooperation
- U visa for victims of qualifying crimes
- Contact the National Domestic Violence Hotline: 1-800-799-7233

## 6. Evidence of Bona Fide Marriage

USCIS wants to see that your marriage is genuine and not entered solely for
immigration benefits. Provide as much of the following as possible:

### Joint financial evidence
- Joint bank account statements
- Joint tax returns (filing "married filing jointly")
- Joint lease or mortgage
- Joint utility bills
- Joint health/auto/life insurance
- Evidence of financial commingling or shared expenses

### Shared life evidence
- Photos together (at events, vacations, with families, over time)
- Correspondence (letters, messages showing genuine relationship)
- Travel records showing trips together
- Evidence of shared children (birth certificates)
- Affidavits from friends and family attesting to the relationship
- Wedding photos and invitations
- Shared subscriptions, memberships

### What USCIS is looking for
- Evidence that spans the duration of your relationship (not just around the wedding)
- Evidence from multiple categories (not just photos)
- Quality over quantity — a few strong pieces of evidence from different categories
  is better than a thick stack of just one type

## 7. Common Issues and Red Flags

### Age difference
Large age gaps between spouses increase scrutiny. Be prepared with extra evidence
of a genuine relationship.

### Prior immigration violations
Overstay, prior denials, prior removal orders — all complicate the case.
The U.S. citizen spouse's petition doesn't erase prior violations.

### Short courtship
Getting married quickly after meeting can raise fraud concerns. Document
your courtship history thoroughly.

### Prior marriages
Both spouses must provide evidence that all prior marriages were legally terminated
(divorce decrees, death certificates, annulment orders).

### Marriage fraud
Immigration marriage fraud is a federal crime. Penalties include fines, imprisonment,
permanent bar from immigration benefits, and removal. USCIS actively investigates
suspected fraud marriages. Do NOT marry someone for immigration purposes — it
will not end well.

### Stokes interview
If USCIS suspects fraud, they may conduct a "Stokes interview" — interviewing each
spouse separately and comparing answers. Be truthful and consistent. Know details
about your daily life together, your home, your routines, and each other's families.
